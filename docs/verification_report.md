## net-metering-cap

**Claim:** Ontario removed the 500 kW size cap from its net metering regulation (O. Reg. 541/05), allowing commercial customers to size solar systems to their load, with monthly excess generation credits that roll over and expire after 12 months.

*(checked by gemini-3-flash-preview)*

**VERDICT: CONFIRMED**

**CORRECTION: none**

**EVIDENCE:** Ontario Regulation 24/17, which amended the Net Metering Regulation (O. Reg. 541/05) effective July 1, 2017, officially revoked the 500 kW capacity limit previously found in section 7(1)(c). This change was intended to allow customers to "right-size" renewable energy systems to their specific load requirements, provided the electricity is generated "primarily for the generator's own use." Under section 8(8) of the current regulation, excess generation credits are carried forward for a rolling period of 12 months, after which any unused credits are reduced to zero.

## clean-tech-itc

**Claim:** Canada's 30% federal Clean Technology Investment Tax Credit can only be claimed by taxable Canadian corporations, so a tax-exempt university like the University of Waterloo cannot claim it directly for a solar installation it owns.

*(checked by gemini-3-flash-preview)*

**VERDICT: PARTLY CONFIRMED**

**CORRECTION:** While the claim is correct regarding universities, the credit is not restricted *only* to taxable Canadian corporations; it can also be claimed by mutual fund trusts that are Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs).

**EVIDENCE:** Under the *Income Tax Act* (as enacted by Bill C-59 in June 2024), the 30% Clean Technology ITC is restricted to "qualifying taxpayers," defined specifically as taxable Canadian corporations and REITs. Tax-exempt entities, including registered charities and public institutions like the University of Waterloo, are ineligible to claim this 30% credit directly and must instead look to the 15% Clean Electricity ITC, which was specifically designed to include certain non-taxable entities. To benefit from the higher 30% rate, tax-exempt institutions must typically enter into complex partnership structures with taxable corporate partners who can claim and monetize the credit.

## clean-electricity-itc

**Claim:** Canada's Clean Electricity Investment Tax Credit is 15%, refundable, and has a broader claimant base than the Clean Technology ITC, potentially including universities or entities controlled by eligible public-sector entities.

*(checked by gemini-3-flash-preview)*

VERDICT: CONFIRMED

CORRECTION: none

EVIDENCE: Canada's Clean Electricity Investment Tax Credit (ITC), enacted via Bill C-15 in early 2026 (Section 127.491 of the Income Tax Act), provides a 15% refundable tax credit for eligible capital costs of clean electricity property acquired on or after April 16, 2024. Unlike the 30% Clean Technology ITC which is generally restricted to taxable Canadian corporations, the Clean Electricity ITC has a broader claimant base that includes tax-exempt entities such as provincial and territorial Crown corporations, municipal-owned corporations, and Indigenous-owned entities. Legal and tax experts confirm that universities and colleges "potentially" qualify for this credit if they are legally structured as or controlled by these eligible public-sector entities, provided the relevant jurisdiction meets federal net-zero commitment requirements.

## om-cost

**Claim:** Operations & maintenance for commercial rooftop solar runs about C$12-18 per kW per year, with snowy climates at the upper end.

*(checked by gemini-3-flash-preview)*

**VERDICT: REFUTED**

**CORRECTION:** Operations & maintenance (O&M) for commercial rooftop solar in Canada typically ranges from **C$20 to C$30 per kW per year**, with snowy climates like Alberta benchmarked at approximately **C$27.47 per kW per year**.

**EVIDENCE:** While the claim's range of C$12–18 might reflect older data or specific "preventative-only" contracts, current Canadian benchmarks are significantly higher; for instance, **Alberta Utilities Commission** data identifies a fixed O&M cost of **C$27.47/kW/year** for commercial solar. International benchmarks from **IRENA (2024)** for OECD countries estimate utility-scale O&M at ~USD 21/kW (~C$29/kW), and commercial rooftop systems—which lack utility-scale economies—regularly exceed these figures. Industry rules of thumb (0.2% to 1% of a C$2.00/W system cost) place the range at **C$4 to C$28 per kW**, meaning the claim's "upper end" of $18 is an underestimate for snowy regions where snow clearing and structural inspections add significant expense.

## snow-losses

**Claim:** Monthly snow losses for fixed rooftop PV in the Ontario snowbelt are roughly 25-30% in January/February and about 5% in summer months.

*(checked by gemini-3-flash-preview)*

VERDICT: PARTLY CONFIRMED

CORRECTION: Snow losses in summer months are 0%; the 5% figure likely refers to annual snow losses or summer soiling/temperature-related efficiency drops.

EVIDENCE: Monthly snow losses for fixed rooftop PV in Ontario can reach 25–30% in January and February, particularly for low-tilt systems (under 20°) in high-snowfall regions like the Muskoka or Grey-Bruce snowbelts. However, there are no snow losses in summer; solar industry standards and NRCan data specify 0% snow loss for these months, though systems may experience 3–5% efficiency losses due to high temperatures or soiling. A five-year study by the Northern Alberta Institute of Technology (NAIT) found that while monthly winter losses are significant, the total annual energy loss due to snow is typically only 3–5% for properly tilted arrays.

## solar-api

**Claim:** Google's Solar API buildingInsights endpoint assumes ~400 W panels by default, its panel counts exclude roof planes that are too steep, north-facing or heavily shaded, and it has a free tier of about 10,000 buildingInsights calls per month.

*(checked by gemini-3-flash-preview)*

The claim is **CONFIRMED**. Google's Solar API `buildingInsights` endpoint uses a default panel wattage of 400 W, excludes unsuitable roof areas from its panel counts based on specific physical and environmental criteria, and provides a free tier of 10,000 calls per month.

### VERDICT: CONFIRMED

### CORRECTION: none

### EVIDENCE:
*   **Default Panel Wattage:** The official Google Solar API methodology documentation explicitly states that "Each panel is assumed to be 400 W with an efficiency of 20.4%" and a DC to AC derate factor of 85%.
*   **Panel Count Exclusions:** The API's `maxArrayPanelsCount` and `solarPanelConfigs` are modeled based on "optimal" placement, which excludes roof segments that are "too small, too shaded, or too steep" (typically defined as having a pitch > 60 degrees or receiving insufficient solar flux). North-facing roof planes in the northern hemisphere are generally excluded or deprioritized because they fall below the minimum sunshine/flux thresholds required for a viable installation.
*   **Free Tier:** Google Maps Platform pricing for the Solar API includes a "free usage cap" of 10,000 `buildingInsights` calls per month; subsequent calls are billed at a tiered rate (e.g., $10 per 1,000 calls for the next 90,000).
